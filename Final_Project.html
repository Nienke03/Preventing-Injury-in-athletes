How can injuries in high level athletes be prevented when looking at sleep quality, stress levels and daily physical activity

To answer this we want to answer these seperate questions:

- Does sleep quality influence injury risk? - Does stress level influence injury risk? - Does bmi influence injury risk?

Research shows that injury risk in high-level athletes is influenced by multiple factors, including sleep, stress, and body composition. According to the American Academy of Clinical Sleep Medicine, athletes who get less than 7-8 hours of sleep per night have a higher risk of injuries, with studies showing up to a 1.7 fold increase. Poor sleep affects recovery, increases stress hormones, and impairs neuromuscular control, making athletes more prone to accidents and injuries. Additionally, elite athletes with better sleep patterns had substantially lower odds of new injuries, and increased daily sleep correlated with a marked reduction in the next day’s injury risk. Practicing healthy sleeping habits leads to health in other areas of life, which then directly lead to health in physical activities, specifically high-level sports. Lowering stress hormones, increasing sleep, and practicing healthy daily habits is necessary to maintain a proper balance in reaching peak physical ability.

Source: https://aacsm.org/sleep-deprivation-and-increased-risk-of-sports-related-injuries/

Finding a balance between staying physically active and maintaining overall health is a challenge that many people face at some point in their life. Creating a schedule that allows time for work, social life, and personal interests – while still prioritizing sleep, stress management, and exercise – can be difficult. People often strive to maintain the perfect balance between physical activity, rest, and low stress levels, but daily responsibilities and unexpected demands always manage to disrupt at least one of these factors. When one variable is significantly altered, how does that shift affect injury risk and is it connected to overall body composition? This dataset attempts to explore those questions by analyzing the responses of participants about their physical activity, sleep, and stress compared to injury risk and bodily fat. By examining these relationships, the analysis aims to better understand how imbalances in daily habits and health may influence both short-term injury risk and long-term health outcomes.

Some limitations our datasets have is the lack of information about who the people being studied are and context around the findings. We do not know where the participants are from and the participants differ in each dataset. The best we can do is average out scores and compare those means to find general patterns. One ethical question we encounter is whether or not these datasets can be compared with each other when we do not know how similar each person actually is.

This dataset is a kaggle dataset produced by the user “mugiwara_46” last updated in November, 2025. It takes 15,420 samples from 156 different athletes measuring different metrics. It measures 6 features of physiological metrics, 8 features of biomechanical data, 4 features of environmental factors, 4 features of workload indicators, and 7 features of metadata columns. Does a person’s daily step count influence injury risk?

This data shows that a lower amount of daily steps would lead to a higher risk of injury. We have to keep in mind that we are looking at data from athletes and that these steps are activities outside their regular training. Apparently extra walking can give the body (and mind) more rest, increasing their health benefits.

Another take on this data could be that athletes that train a lot take fewer daily steps (because they are exhausted) and will be injured faster. To back this conclusion up we have to look into more data: does walking only help in certain sports or can this be generalized? Is it the daily steps that help the lower injury or is it the exhaustion from the workout that determines this?

Major Takeaways

Based on the visualizations, it is clear that health in all aspects of life is incredibly important towards staying healthy as a high-level athlete. Taking care of your body in sleep, stress, and daily exercise, outside of training, is integral towards lowering the risk of injury. As we began this research we knew that keeping a strong mentality and taking care of the little things is important both for athletes and people trying to stay physically active, but this data helped visualize just how important each step is. Adding another hour each night to a sleep schedule, or not allowing extra distractions to get in the way of priorities and adding stress to an already busy and stressful schedule, can make the difference in a practice or game. Allowing the body to perform at the level required for high performance means taking care of it in the hours, days, and weeks before, and eliminating potential risk outside of the sport itself. Being an athlete at any level requires incredible talent, work, and dedication to the actual craft of the game, but beyond that people must understand how it all ties together. Teaching athletes how to do this will only improve their abilities and keep them on the field of play for longer in their career. This data shows three different ways that athletes’ health is protected by healthy habits, showing similar correlation in all three ways. Keeping an organized, productive, and overall healthy lifestyle will lead to positive outcomes and a lower risk of injury in high-level athletes careers.

Conclusions about bias in data exploration

One of our samples consists of athletes and the other of people from the general population. This could lead to some bias. We grouped the data on sleep quality and took the mean occurred injury data from the athlete sample and the activity level, stress and daily steps from the sample of the general population. There is a big chance that the stress level or step count of an athlete that sleeps the same as a non-athlete is totally different. It is unsure to say if our data is overestimating or underestimating, but the chance that it contains bias is evident.

Future Directions

To pursue a better answer to our big picture question we would like to expand our data and survey significantly more people, like setting up a cohort study. Having access to high level athletes and their biometric data, injury history, and strength would be very helpful. Besides that, we want to make sure that our findings are directly correlated. For example, does taking 2000 less steps a day lead to lower injury risk or is it the exhaustion from the workout that makes athletes take 2000 steps less?
